Big Moves

OpenAI's First Custom Chip Is Here — and It's Called Jalapeño

OpenAI has unveiled Jalapeño, its first in-house AI inference processor, built in partnership with Broadcom. The chip is an ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit) designed specifically for the unique demands of OpenAI's inference systems — the layer of the stack you interact with every time you use ChatGPT.

For years, AI companies have rented compute from Nvidia. A proprietary chip shifts the cost structure fundamentally. Jalapeño signals that OpenAI is serious about long-term margin control, and that will ripple into pricing for enterprise customers. If inference costs drop — even gradually — the economics of large-scale AI deployments improve, but it also means OpenAI becomes less dependent on, and potentially more competitive with, its cloud partners.

What should I do as a leader? Track your enterprise AI cost baselines now. As vendors move to custom silicon, expect pricing shifts — potentially downward for inference-heavy workloads — over the next 12–18 months. Locking in long-term contracts before that clarity arrives may not serve you.

Google Embeds Computer Control Directly Into Gemini 3.5 Flash

Google has integrated "Computer Use" directly into Gemini 3.5 Flash, giving the model the ability to see and operate computers, browsers, and mobile devices on its own. On the OSWorld benchmark — a standard test for autonomous desktop control — it scores 78.4, putting it on par with GPT-5.5. Developers can use the Gemini API to build agents for software testing, office automation, and similar workflows.

Computer use is the gateway to fully autonomous software agents. When an AI can see your screen, navigate graphical interfaces, fill forms, and trigger workflows without a dedicated API integration, it opens the door to automating tasks that previously required custom development. That is a compelling capability — and a new attack surface.

What should I do as a leader? If your security and compliance teams have not reviewed agentic AI policies, computer use capabilities make that review urgent. The potential for unintended data access expands significantly when an AI model can operate software directly, outside of any structured API boundary.

What should I do? If you are a developer, the Gemini API now allows you to build agents for browser-based and desktop automation tasks without specialized wrappers or virtual machine tooling.

The Token Budget Crisis: Enterprises Are Rationing AI Use

Companies are discovering that giving employees access to AI tools without usage guardrails leads to runaway spending. The pattern is consistent: employees default to powerful, expensive models for routine tasks that a simpler tool would handle adequately. Enterprises are now implementing hard token limits — a practice that wasn't on most IT governance roadmaps a year ago.

The story of enterprise AI adoption is frequently one of enthusiasm outpacing governance. A single complex query to a GPT-4 class model can cost significantly more than its simpler alternatives. Without usage policies, costs scale non-linearly with adoption — and the finance team eventually notices.

What should I do as a leader? Establish model-tier guidelines: lightweight models for lookups and drafts, more capable (and expensive) models for complex reasoning tasks. Pair this with usage monitoring and a feedback loop so employees understand the tradeoff they are making.

What should I do? Before reaching for the most powerful model available, ask whether the task actually requires heavy reasoning. Most do not.
