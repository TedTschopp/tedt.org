AWS Launches Two Services to Give AI Agents Context and Security

At its New York summit, AWS announced Continuum and Context — two services designed to close the gap between what AI agents can do and what enterprises can responsibly deploy. Continuum automatically detects, prioritizes, and remediates code vulnerabilities. Context builds a knowledge graph from corporate data so that AI agents can work with actual business context rather than hallucinating organizational facts.

Why it matters: Both services address the most persistent complaints about enterprise agentic AI: agents that write fast but break security, and agents that sound confident while lacking any grounding in what the organization actually does. AWS is positioning its platform as the governance and grounding layer for enterprise AI — a signal that the market is moving from "AI does things" to "AI does the right things, with guardrails."

What should I do as a leader? Evaluate Continuum and Context as potential accelerators for your AI governance roadmap, particularly if your teams are building agentic workflows and spending significant effort on custom context injection or manual security review.

What should I do? If you are on an AWS-based AI engineering team, add these services to your next architecture review. Context's knowledge graph approach is worth comparing to how your team currently grounds agent context in enterprise data.

"Human in the Loop" Is Not a Quality Standard

A widely circulated post on Age of Product makes a pointed argument: calling something "human in the loop" says nothing about quality unless it is paired with specific, measurable criteria for what the human is checking and what failure looks like. Many enterprise teams are using human review as a way to defer accountability rather than establish it.

Why it matters: When something goes wrong with AI-assisted work, "a human reviewed it" is not a sufficient defense if there was no defined standard for what they were reviewing against. Vague oversight processes create compliance exposure, not compliance protection — and that distinction will matter when auditors and regulators ask the questions.

What should I do as a leader? Before your next AI deployment, define AI-specific acceptance criteria: what does the reviewer check, against what criteria, and what is the remediation path when a check fails? "Human reviews output" should expand into a documented, auditable checklist.

What should I do? If you are using AI-generated output in your daily work, ask yourself: do you have a mental checklist for what you verify before passing it on? If the answer is no, you are functioning as a rubber stamp, not a quality gate.

The Case Against LLM-Written Incident Reports

A practitioner post from Surfing Complexity raises a concern that resonates broadly in enterprise operations: if incident reports are written by LLMs, they will be well-structured and readable — and potentially hollow. The author argues that incident reporting derives much of its value from the friction of writing, not from the clarity of the finished document. An LLM can produce a coherent post-mortem that obscures the organizational lessons a human author would have been forced to confront.

Why it matters: Incident reports are a primary learning mechanism for resilient operations. If AI drafts them, the output may pass review while missing the tacit knowledge and pattern recognition that make incident analysis valuable. This concern extends to any AI-assisted documentation of failure: root-cause analyses, security incident reports, compliance filings.

What should I do as a leader? Set explicit policy on where AI may assist in incident documentation and where human-authored narrative is required. At minimum, require that the "contributing factors" and "what we will change" sections of any incident report are drafted by a human participant who was involved — not generated from a transcript summary.
