one_line_definition: Claiming one small step will automatically lead to a long chain of extreme bad results.
plain_english: >-
  Slippery Slope says that if one thing happens, a much bigger and worse outcome will surely follow. Sometimes small changes do create bigger effects, so the idea is not always wrong. The bug appears when the chain is assumed instead of proved. People skip the middle steps, ignore guardrails, and act like no one can stop the slide once it starts. A good test is to ask what specific step links the first event to the final disaster, and what would stop the chain before it gets there.

  - If we make one exception for a customer, every client will control our product.
sounds_like_real_life:

  - If we say yes to one small favor, people will walk over us forever.
fiction_examples:

    summary: Characters treat one political move as if it must lead straight to tyranny or total ruin.
    why_it_fits: Fear of the future skips over all the uncertain middle steps.
  - source: Fairy tale warnings
    summary: A small choice is often framed as the first step toward total disaster, even when the story leaves out the path between.
    why_it_fits: The chain is dramatic, but not always argued clearly.
  - source: Frankenstein

    why_it_fits: The imagined future becomes more certain than the actual evidence.
why_it_happens: >-
  Slippery slope claims are powerful because fear moves fast. A dramatic future can shut down careful discussion in the present.
warning_signs:
  - A tiny change gets linked to a huge disaster with no clear bridge.
  - The middle steps stay vague.
  - Guardrails, policies, and human choices are ignored.
  - The future is described as certain long before the evidence supports that certainty.
response_tips:
  - What exact steps connect the first move to that final outcome?
  - What would stop the chain before it gets that far?
  - Are we describing a real risk, or jumping to the most dramatic ending?
  - What evidence shows this always leads to that?
