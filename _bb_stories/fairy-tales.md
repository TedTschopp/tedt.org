title: Fairy Tales
story_family: Story Source Guide
lead: Fairy tales shrink big thinking mistakes into short scenes that are easy to spot and hard to forget.
why_this_source: Fairy tales work well for Brain Bugs because the scenes are simple, the stakes are clear, and the warning is usually visible right away. That makes them useful for younger readers and first-time learners.
notice_points:
  - Notice how fast crowds copy each other.
  - Watch for strange choices that feel forced into only one path.
  - Look for familiar warnings that people repeat without checking.
featured_errors:
  - bandwagon-fallacy
  - social-proof-bias
  - black-and-white-thinking
  - familiarity-heuristic
related_comparisons:
  - fallacies-vs-biases
  - groupthink-vs-social-proof-bias
story_spotlights:
  - source: The Emperor's New Clothes
    title: The crowd protects the lie because nobody wants to stand alone.
    summary: People copy the public reaction around them instead of checking what they can plainly see for themselves.
    why_it_matters: This is one of the clearest story examples of bandwagon pressure and social proof.
    errors:
      - bandwagon-fallacy
      - social-proof-bias
  - source: Beauty and the Beast
    title: First impressions and harsh labels can hide what is really there.
    summary: Characters sort people into simple good-or-bad boxes before they understand the full person or situation.
    why_it_matters: Fairy tales often use fast moral labels, which makes them useful for teaching black-and-white thinking and halo effects.
    errors:
      - black-and-white-thinking
      - halo-effect
  - source: Fairy tale warnings passed down again and again
    title: Repetition can make a rule feel true even when the situation changes.
    summary: Some warnings stay powerful because people hear them so often that they start to feel like automatic truth.
    why_it_matters: This helps learners see how familiarity can feel like evidence.
    errors:
      - familiarity-heuristic
      - appeal-to-emotion
