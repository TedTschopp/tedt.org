title: Shakespeare
story_family: Story Source Guide
lead: Shakespeare is full of pressure, misreading, dramatic language, and bad calls made in public.
why_this_source: Shakespeare is useful because his scenes put persuasion, pride, fear, and crowd pressure right on the stage. You can hear the Brain Bug in the way characters talk, not just in what happens next.
notice_points:
  - Listen for speeches that move feelings faster than facts.
  - Notice when a character reacts to a distorted version of the claim.
  - Watch how public pressure changes what people say and believe.
featured_errors:
  - appeal-to-emotion
  - loaded-language
  - emotional-reasoning
  - hindsight-bias
related_comparisons:
  - appeal-to-emotion-vs-loaded-language
  - whataboutism-vs-tu-quoque
story_spotlights:
  - source: Julius Caesar
    title: A crowd can be moved with careful emotion and loaded words.
    summary: The funeral speeches steer the public by making people feel outrage, pity, and urgency before they slow down to test the reasoning.
    why_it_matters: This is a strong teaching scene for appeal to emotion and loaded language because the persuasion happens out loud.
    errors:
      - appeal-to-emotion
      - loaded-language
  - source: Hamlet
    title: Strong feelings get treated like proof.
    summary: Hamlet often treats a painful emotion or suspicion as if it settles what is true, even when the facts are still incomplete.
    why_it_matters: That makes the play useful for teaching emotional reasoning and distraction through red herrings.
    errors:
      - emotional-reasoning
      - red-herring
  - source: Romeo and Juliet
    title: Looking back after disaster makes the warning signs feel obvious.
    summary: Once the tragedy is clear, readers and characters can feel as if the ending should have been easy to predict all along.
    why_it_matters: This helps explain hindsight bias in a memorable way.
    errors:
      - hindsight-bias
      - false-dilemma
