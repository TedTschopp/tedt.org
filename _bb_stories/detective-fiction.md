title: Detective Fiction
story_family: Story Source Guide
lead: Mystery stories are perfect for teaching weak clues, false causes, overconfidence, and the risk of locking onto one theory too early.
why_this_source: Detective stories feel logical, which makes them excellent training ground for bad reasoning. Readers and characters both want a fast answer, and that pressure can turn a clue into a false certainty.
notice_points:
  - Ask whether one clue is being treated like a complete case.
  - Notice when a vivid detail feels more important than the base rate.
  - Watch for side trails that distract from the main question.
featured_errors:
  - confirmation-bias
  - correlation-vs-causation
  - base-rate-neglect
  - red-herring
  - illusion-of-explanatory-depth
related_comparisons:
  - base-rate-neglect-vs-availability-heuristic
  - overconfidence-vs-illusion-of-explanatory-depth
story_spotlights:
  - source: Sherlock Holmes stories
    title: A good detective story also shows what weak detection looks like.
    summary: The best Holmes stories highlight how easy it is for other characters to settle on the wrong clue, the wrong cause, or the first theory that feels tidy.
    why_it_matters: That contrast makes detective fiction ideal for teaching confirmation bias, false cause, and red herrings.
    errors:
      - confirmation-bias
      - correlation-vs-causation
      - red-herring
  - source: Dracula and gothic investigation scenes
    title: Fear can make rare outcomes feel common.
    summary: Characters react to alarming clues and strange behavior in ways that can crowd out the slower question of what is most likely.
    why_it_matters: That makes the source helpful for teaching base-rate neglect and fear-driven reasoning.
    errors:
      - base-rate-neglect
      - availability-heuristic
  - source: Detective stories with early theory lock-in
    title: Knowing a little can feel like knowing enough.
    summary: Once someone explains a few clues, they can start to feel more certain than the evidence really allows.
    why_it_matters: This is a useful pattern for teaching the illusion of explanatory depth.
    errors:
      - illusion-of-explanatory-depth
      - overconfidence-effect
