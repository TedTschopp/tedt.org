## Q: What does IT look like in the future given the business-led development model and AI-enhanced solution development?

IT in the future looks less like a centralized shop that hand-builds every
solution itself, and more like an industrialized platform organization that
makes distributed delivery safe, repeatable, and scalable. AI will allow
business teams to create workflows, apps, automations, analytics, and copilots
much faster. But for a regulated utility, speed only creates value when there 
is quality in the form of secure, supportable, auditable, and operationally 
reliable solutions. That means IT’s role becomes more strategic, not less.

The right model is for IT to build the factory, not to personally operate every
machine in it. IT should design the platforms, certify the patterns, engineer
the controls, and maintain the production environment. The business users, who
are closest to the work, should then be able to use those approved tools and
patterns to solve local problems safely. That is the core shift: IT moves from
being only a builder of individual solutions to being the designer and operator
of the system of systems or factory through which many solutions can be built.

This also means IT’s R&D responsibility expands. Before a pattern is broadly
deployed by the business, IT needs to prove that it is safe and supportable.
That includes building and validating reusable templates, approved connectors,
secure data access paths, AI guardrails, monitoring standards, deployment
pipelines, lifecycle controls, and support models. The business should not be
inventing its own ways to build and deploy digital capabilities in production.
IT should do that industrialization work first, then make those capabilities
broadly consumable.

This is how mature industries operate. In pharma and medicine, innovation is not
enough on its own; the method has to be validated, controlled, and safe before
it is used at scale. In automotive, central engineering does not ask every plant
or operator across the globe to invent its own tooling, tolerances, safety practices, 
or quality checks. Engineering designs the platform and the production system; 
the factory operates within it. That is the right mental model here as well. 
IT is the engineering and factory function. The business are the skilled operators within
that system.

That is also why the current governance-heavy posture is correct. At this stage,
governance is seen as bureacracy when in reality governance is about quality.  
And we are not building governance for its own sake; it is that quality that we are after so scale production and value. 
Over time, the goal is standard work products that have quality embededed directoy into the platforms and patters so that quality becomes the automated fast way towards done.  all of this is so that that delivery becomes safer, easier, faster, and more repeatable.

The practical end state is a tiered model. Team-level productivity solutions can
be built by the business on approved platforms. Departmental solutions can be
delivered within a managed model with stronger review and lifecycle controls.
Enterprise-critical, customer-impacting, grid-impacting, or compliance-relevant
systems remain under professional IT engineering and DevSecOps discipline.
Future-state IT is therefore a platform engineering, architecture, security, and
industrialization function that enables the business to move faster without
creating shadow IT or unmanaged risk.

**Q: Do we want non-IT org units to hire developers?**

As a general rule, no. We want business units to have strong digital capability, but we do not want each organization creating its own independent software engineering function outside IT. The business absolutely should have product owners, analysts, process experts, and advanced users who can configure approved platforms and partner closely with IT. But general-purpose software engineering, integration, security, and production support should remain under IT leadership.

The reason is simple: once separate developer groups proliferate in the business, we create shadow IT. That leads to fragmented standards, inconsistent controls, duplicated skills, harder support, more vendor sprawl, and higher cyber and operational risk. Where a business area has sustained demand for technical delivery, the better answer is embedded IT resources or cross-functional product teams aligned to that business domain, not a separate developer organization reporting outside IT. Exceptions may exist for highly specialized technical roles, but they should be rare, justified, and governed as exceptions by leadership in IT rather than becoming the default model.

**Q: What is our stance beyond Power Platform (Palantir, Snowflake, Salesforce)?**

Our stance should be a default platform model with governed exceptions. Power Platform should be the supported default for business-led solutions: lightweight apps, workflow automation, forms, approvals, and productivity-oriented extensions. That gives us a clear support model, common guardrails, reusable patterns, and a simpler operating model for the majority of business-led use cases.

Palantir, Snowflake, and Salesforce remain important, but for defined purposes rather than as alternative low-code ecosystems. Palantir should stay focused on the operational data integration and emergency-response scenarios it was intended to support. Snowflake is our data lakehouse and analytics foundation, not our default application platform. Salesforce should continue to serve out its useful life, while its footprint is rationalized alongside SAP and other enterprise platforms over time. These platforms do overlap with Power Platform at the edges, so any use of them for business-led solution development should be exception-based, architecture-reviewed, and justified by a capability gap that cannot be met effectively on the supported platform. The goal is to reduce overlap, not normalize parallel development stacks.

## Q: What does this mean for Data Science and the data scientists in the business?

Historically, data scientists in the business have had a high degree of
autonomy. That autonomy helped accelerate experimentation and local insight
generation, but it also led to fragmented tooling, inconsistent support, uneven
security, and models that were difficult to operationalize, monitor, or scale.
That model is no longer sufficient for an enterprise that wants to use data
science and AI safely at scale.

Going forward, data scientists should still remain close to the business
problem, because the value of data science depends heavily on domain
understanding. What needs to change is not their proximity to the work, but the
foundation on which they work. Their tooling, environments, data access
patterns, and deployment paths need to align to platforms that are supported,
secured, and operationalized by IT. That is what enables safe data science.

In practical terms, that means business data scientists should be able to
generate insights locally and move quickly, but within enterprise-supported
environments, approved data products, governed access controls, and standard
paths to production. When work remains exploratory, the controls can be lighter.
As soon as a model begins to drive decisions, automation, customer outcomes,
operational processes, or regulatory reporting, the expectations should increase
materially. At that point the work must be supportable, monitored, explainable
where appropriate, and aligned to enterprise standards.

This is not about stripping autonomy away from data scientists. It is about
moving from individual autonomy to enterprise-grade capability. The goal is
safe, supported, and scalable data science that still delivers local insight,
but also strengthens the enterprise as a whole through reuse, consistency,
security, and operational maturity.

**Q: Business-led development is a poor name. IT is also part of the business.**

That concern is valid. IT is part of the business, so “business-led development” can sound like IT is somehow outside the enterprise, which is not the intent. The real distinction is not between “the business” and IT. The distinction is between local, domain-driven solution creation and centrally governed enterprise engineering.

A better term would be **Federated Solution Delivery** or something along those lines. It is more precise and avoids the baggage of “citizen development,” which is both imprecise and increasingly distracting in a global workforce. Whatever label we use, the definition should be explicit: business domains help shape and improve local solutions on approved platforms, while IT owns the enterprise platforms, security, architecture, integration, and production governance. The name matters less than making the operating model clear, but a more accurate name will reduce confusion.

## Q: What does this mean for cost?

This model should improve time-to-value and reduce the unit cost of smaller
solutions, but only if we manage it with real investment discipline.
Benefit-cost ratios still matter, but they are only as good as the data that
supports them. Today, some of that data is not being collected consistently,
which means we are making investment decisions without a complete picture of
value, cost, or tradeoffs. That has to change.

We need a more disciplined portfolio view that captures expected benefits,
expected costs, and realized outcomes. That includes not only build effort, but
also adoption, productivity gains, cycle-time reduction, error reduction,
contractor avoidance, compliance value, licensing, integration cost, support
burden, and lifecycle cost. AI and distributed development will dramatically
increase the number of ideas that can be turned into solutions. That makes
financial discipline more important, not less.

There is also an important capability issue here. To make this model work, we
need to grow beyond treating digital work purely as overhead inside a regulated
utility mindset and bring more of the discipline of a normal business operating
to a P&L. That does not mean ignoring the realities of the regulatory
environment. It means becoming much better at understanding where value is
created, what it costs to sustain, which investments deserve to scale, and which
ones should stop.

Many people do not yet have those skills because they have not needed them in
the past. Historically, they were not asked to estimate benefits rigorously,
compare investment alternatives, manage demand against constrained capacity, or
track realized value after deployment. That means training is not optional; it
is part of the cost model. We need to build stronger capability in product
thinking, financial analysis, value realization, prioritization, and lifecycle
management across both IT and the business.

There will be some near-term cost to do this well: training, platform hardening,
governance automation, support model design, and change management. But those
are foundational investments, not administrative overhead. Without them, we will
create more solutions but also more duplication, more orphaned assets, more
support burden, and more unmanaged risk. The goal is not simply to spend less.
The goal is to produce more measurable business value per dollar invested, with
greater visibility into what is working, what is not, and where the next dollar
should go.

**Q: What about IT developers in the business?**

The existence of an HR-recognized role should be understood as a governance backstop, not a hiring target. Its purpose is to give us a formal mechanism when a role crosses the line from occasional business-led solution work into sustained technical delivery. It was not intended to encourage the business to build permanent developer positions outside IT.

The core principle is that federated delivery works when many people contribute incremental digital improvement as part of their primary role. It stops working when a few individuals in the business become de facto full-time developers and begin to concentrate technical ownership outside IT. If someone is spending more than roughly half of their time building applications, automations, data pipelines, or technical solutions, that is usually a signal that the operating model needs to change. At that point, the work should either move into IT, be supported by embedded IT resources, or be organized as a formal cross-functional product team. We want broad participation in digital transformation. We do not want shadow IT, where technical capability becomes isolated in the business without the governance, support model, or career framework needed to sustain it.

---

The consistent message across all of these answers is that we are not decentralizing accountability. We are expanding delivery capacity through a governed, federated model: default platforms, clear exceptions, shared data foundations, centralized controls, and defined role boundaries. That is how a utility gets more speed without taking on more unmanaged risk.
